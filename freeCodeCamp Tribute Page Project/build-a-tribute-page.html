Harriet Tubman

One of the most important and influential activists in American history

March 1822: Born in Dorchester County, Maryland.

Late 1830s: Tubman experienced an accidental, severe head injury after an overseer threw a weight meant for another enslaved person and missed, hitting her in the head. This led to lifelong health issues including seizures and horrible headaches.

1844: She married a free Black man named John Tubman.

1849: She escaped enslavement and returned to Maryland to rescue her family. This is also when she began rescuing other enslaved people.

December 1850: Tubman helped her niece and her family avoid being sold away and escape to Philadelphia.

1858: She began working with fellow abolitionist, John Brown. Tubman helped him plan a raid on Harpers Ferry, Virginia.

1863: She became "the first woman to lead an armed assault during the Civil War".

1874: A bill was introduced in the House of Representatives to pay Tubman "the sum of $2,000 for services rendered by her to the Union Army as scout, nurse, and spy", but it unfortunately did not pass in the Senate.

Late 1890s: Tubman got involved in women's suffrage, traveling around the country and speaking out in support.

March 10, 1913: Passed away in Auburn, New York.
